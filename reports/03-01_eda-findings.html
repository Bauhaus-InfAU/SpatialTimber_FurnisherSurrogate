Exploratory Data Analysis: Findings Report

Project: FurnisherSurrogate surrogate model for furniture placement quality scores

Dataset: 8,322 apartments / 45,880 active rooms from the SpatialTimber Furnisher

Date: February 2026

1. Dataset Overview

The dataset contains procedurally generated apartment layouts evaluated by a furnisher algorithm. Each apartment has 39 rooms; each room receives an independent placement quality score (0100).

Metric Value
Apartments 8,322
Active rooms 45,880
Apartment types 7 (Studio through 5-Bedroom)
Room types 9 (Bedroom, Living room, Bathroom, WC, Kitchen, Children 14)
Score range 0100 (integer-like, 0 = complete failure)
Polygon format Closed 2D polylines, 48 unique vertices

Sanity checks passed: no missing scores, all polygons closed, all doors on wall, all scores in [0, 100]. Vertex counts are exclusively 4, 6, or 8 corresponding to rectangles, L-shapes, and more complex notched rooms.

  • 28.6% of rooms fail completely (score = 0); 41.6% score 90 or above. Only 3.3% land in the 139 range.

8. Implications for Modelling

8.1 Target distribution

The bimodal score distribution (28.6% zeros, 41.6% above 90, sparse middle) poses a challenge for standard regression. Options:

  1. Two-stage model: classify fail/pass first (strong area signal), then regress score for non-failed rooms.
  2. Single regression trained on the full range, accepting that the zero-spike will dominate early training.
  3. Weighted loss that up-weights the sparse mid-range to prevent the model from collapsing to a bimodal predictor.

8.2 Feature signal

  • Room type is the strongest axis of variation drives both the score distribution shape and the area range.
  • Area is the best single numeric predictor (r = +0.37) and explains nearly all failures below type-specific thresholds.
  • Shape complexity (vertex count) is a strong secondary signal (8-vertex rooms: median score 37 vs 92 for rectangles).
  • Door position has no linear predictive power in normalised form, but its spatial relationship to the room shape may matter this requires rasterised input.
  • Aspect ratio has minimal discriminative power (91.6% of rooms are near-square).
  • Apartment type has a large effect on Living room (eta-sq=0.19, up to 56-point median delta) and Kitchen (eta-sq=0.11, 25-point delta). See 03-03_apartment_type_eda.ipynb for the full analysis.

8.3 Modelling strategy

  • A tabular baseline (e.g., gradient-boosted trees on the 21-dim feature vector: 5 numeric + 9 room type + 7 apartment type) should comfortably beat the naive MAE of 37.48, primarily by learning the areafailure relationship and room-type-specific patterns.
  • A CNN on rasterised room images (Phase 4) can potentially capture shapedoor interactions and local geometry details that summary statistics miss. The weak linear correlations suggest substantial non-linear signal remains unexploited.
  • The children's room scoring ceiling (never above ~76) means the model may need to learn type-specific score ranges rather than a universal 0100 mapping.

9. Data Limitations

If we decide to augment or extend the training data, these limitations should be addressed:

9.1 Geometric constraints

  • Only axis-aligned polygons. All walls are horizontal or vertical. No diagonal walls, curved boundaries, or irregular angles.
  • Only 4, 6, or 8 vertices. No rooms with 3, 5, 7, or >8 vertices exist in the dataset.
  • Counter-clockwise winding order is assumed throughout. Clockwise or mixed-winding polygons are untested.

9.2 Score characteristics

  • Children's room scores are capped around 76.2. The furnisher's scoring function appears to have a structural ceiling for these room types. The model cannot learn to predict scores above this ceiling.
  • WC and Kitchen are nearly binary (0 or 100). Mid-range scores are extremely rare, so the model has almost no training signal for intermediate quality in these types.
  • Score = 0 is ambiguous: it means "complete failure" but the dataset does not distinguish between different failure modes (e.g., room too small for any furniture vs. furniture placed but scoring function returned 0).

9.3 Single door per room

Every room has exactly one door. Rooms with multiple doors, no doors, or wider openings (e.g., archways) are not represented.

9.4 Limited contextual features

  • Apartment type is now included as a feature (7 categories). It has a large effect on Living room and Kitchen scores but negligible effect on other room types. See 03-03_apartment_type_eda.ipynb for the analysis.
  • Other contextual information (neighbouring rooms, corridors, windows, floor-level context) is not available. The inter-room correlation (r = 0.006) suggests broader context doesn't matter for this furnisher.

9.5 Apartment type distribution

  • 2-Bedroom apartments are the most common (25%), 5-Bedroom the rarest (7.7%).
  • Children 4 rooms appear in only 637 instances (1.4% of rooms) predictions for this type will have higher uncertainty.

What is IN the distribution

The model can be expected to perform well on rooms that meet all of the following:

  • Room type is one of the 9 known types
  • Polygon is axis-aligned, closed, with 4, 6, or 8 unique vertices
  • Area falls within the type-specific range shown above (e.g., Bedroom: 3.4\u201327.4 m\u00b2)
  • Aspect ratio is between 1.0 and ~2.6
  • Exactly one door, located on a polygon edge
  • Counter-clockwise winding order

What is OUT of the distribution

The model's predictions should not be trusted for:

  • Non-axis-aligned geometry: diagonal walls, curved walls, irregular angles
  • Unusual vertex counts: 3, 5, 7, or >8 vertices
  • Area outside observed range: e.g., a 40 m\u00b2 bedroom (max observed: 27.4) or a 0.2 m\u00b2 WC (min observed: 0.4)
  • High aspect ratio: rooms with AR > 2.6 are extrapolation territory
  • Unknown room types: any type not in the 9 known categories
  • Multiple doors or no door
  • Very large apartments (>9 rooms): the dataset only covers Studio through 5-Bedroom

Grey zones (limited coverage)

  • Children 4 (n=637): predictions will be less reliable due to limited training examples
  • 8-vertex rooms (n=5,309): under-represented compared to rectangles (n=28,062)
  • Mid-range scores for WC and Kitchen: almost no training examples exist between 1\u201399
  • Rooms near area boundaries: edge cases at the min/max of each type's area range
